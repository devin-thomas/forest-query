Global deforestation in clear terms.

ForestQuery analyzes forest change from 1990 to 2016 to show where the world is losing tree cover, which regions are slipping backward, and which countries deserve the most urgent attention.

This page summarizes the full SQL project with the same evidence used in the written report, but in a cleaner, faster-reading format built for the web.

Most Important Finding

1.32M sq km

The world lost 1,324,449 square kilometers of forest area between 1990 and 2016, a 3.21% decline.

That loss is roughly comparable to the 2016 land area of Peru, which makes the scale of deforestation much easier to grasp.

Why Nigeria deserves special attention

Nigeria is the only country that lands in the top five for both absolute forest loss and percent forest loss. That makes it a high-priority case for ForestQuery because the scale of decline and the severity of decline are both visible in the same country.

  • Scale problem 106,506 square kilometers of forest area lost from 1990 to 2016.
  • Severity problem 61.80% percent decrease, placing Nigeria among the steepest drops in the dataset.
  • Regional context Its decline also reinforces the broader concern inside Sub-Saharan Africa.

Recommended Action

The report's recommendations are strongest when they connect global scale, regional momentum, and country-specific urgency. These are the clearest next moves suggested by the evidence.

Priority countries

  • Brazil and Indonesia Focus on these countries for their outsized contribution to absolute forest loss.
  • Nigeria and Tanzania Use these as frontline priority cases where regional decline and country-level losses overlap.
  • China and Iceland Study these as positive outliers to identify strategies worth adapting elsewhere.

Regional strategy

ForestQuery should concentrate communications, partnerships, and resources in Latin America & Caribbean and Sub-Saharan Africa. These were the only non-world regions that lost forest share over the period, and they contain several of the countries with the most serious losses.

A web summary cannot replace the full report, but it can make the logic of the recommendation much easier to absorb at a glance.
